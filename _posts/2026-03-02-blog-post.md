“Is chain-of-thought reasoning of LLMs a mirage? A data distribution lens”

To counter the criticisms that Large Language Models (LLMs), the architectures which to varying degrees power chatbots like chatGPT or Claude, are merely “stochastic parrots”,
chaining words together based on statistical patterns derived from the enormous corpus of texts they are trained on rather than on a genuine underlying understanding of their meaning, many efforts have been made to endow them with reasoning abilities mimicking our own. One strategy from the user’s end is known as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting which consists in explicitly asking the LLM to e.g. “think step by step”, in the hope that it will imitate the kind of deliberate reasoning examples that can be found in their training data. Implicitly, users are hoping that the abstract rules of human reasoning might somehow be derived from these examples and transferred to this novel question.

This paper does not directly answer the question of whether this strategy is successful in generating human-like reasoning, although the authors’ opinion on the matter seems clear enough, but instead it introduces a toy environment dubbed DATAALCHEMY in which to test the generalisation of reasoning in candidate models beyond what they have seen in training. They do in fact demonstrate the use of DATAALCHEMY on a GPT-2-style model, but this model is far more primitive than those used in current state-of-the-art chatbots.
To study the dependence of CoT prompting on the original dataset, the researchers replace human-generated chains-of-thoughts with all their inherent and unavoidable variability, by a simplified but abstract scheme of “input element + transformation gives an output element”. Elements consist of fixed-length strings of letters (e.g. AAFQ); transformations are symbolic instructions for how to transform these strings, with two transformations tested: F1 which shifts all letters by 13 characters in the alphabet (e.g. AAFQ [F1] → NNSD) and F2 which rotates letters in the element (AAFQ [F2] → QAAF), and can be applied sequentially (e.g. AAFQ [F1][F2] → NNSD [F2] → DNNS). In this scheme, elements would be equivalent to individual problem instances and transformations to a specific logical task in a real-world query, or in other words, elements represent the stuff on which you’re asking the model to reason, and transformations the kind of operation you request. For instance, the elements could be a list of items and prices, and the task could be to work out the bill. As a user, you would hope the model to be able to reply accurately whether you test it with a different list of items (element change) or a different question like sorting the list alphabetically (transformation change). In DATAALCHEMY, the real-world messiness of human words and questions is replaced in terms of these elements and transformations, and models are trained by being shown multiple examples of input element – transformation – output element before being tested on new combinations. To illustrate the fragility of CoT outside the training data distribution, the researchers manipulate three different aspects or dimensions to test how well the model generalises to cases not directly experienced. I will primarily focus on the first of these dimensions and only briefly describe the other two.

This first dimension, called task generalisation, is the ability to apply reasoning patterns to novel cases. Cases can be described as novel in two ways: either the element in the prompt is not in the training set, or the transformation is not. Starting with transformation generalisation, after training the model on elements of four letters and transformations requiring two steps (e.g. AAFQ [F1][F2], etc.), four levels of generalisation are tested: In-Distribution (ID) in which the transformations in the test were already present in the training, Composition (CMP) which tests novel combinations of individual transformations which had been encountered in the training (e.g. the model has never seen [F2][F2] but has seen [F1][F2]), Partially-Out-Of-Distribution (POOD) in which at least one individual transformation has not been seen (e.g. the model has seen [F1] but never [F2] and is being asked to carry out [F2][F1]) and Out-of-Distribution (OOD) in which the transformations are entirely novel (e.g asking it to do [F2][F2] despite it never seeing [F2]). While it may be unsurprising that the tested model fails in the last two cases (indeed how could you expect it to know how a novel instruction like F2 is meant to work without ever seeing it?), the striking result is how catastrophically bad the model was in CMP tests where every individual operations like F1 and F2 had been seen in training but the combination is new. This is exactly the kind of generalisation you might hope from a reasoning system, and it fails to manifest. More worrying still are the results from element generalisation, which the researchers again test to varying levels of shifts: ID in which elements in the test set were all seen in training, CMP in which novel elements are obtained by scrambling elements from training, and OOD in which letters that have never been seen are introduced. While the model could be forgiven in the last case (having no notion of the alphabet, it cannot be expected to know how to substitute unknown letters if prompted with F1 although it might be able to perform F2), its terrible performance in CMP on the other hand is unforgivable as it indicates the model has failed to learn an element-agnostic rule like “rotate the letters in any given word”, instead relying on specific patterns seen in training.

The second dimension DATAALCHEMY can manipulate is length generalisation, which can once again mean either generalising to tasks with elements of different length or to tasks with a different number of reasoning steps. So, if, as previously, the model has been trained to manipulate 4-letter words using two successive steps, how well does it respond to queries involving elements with fewer or more letters or to queries requiring a single or three transformation steps? The GPT-2 model again performs very badly, suggesting that its “reasoning” does not scale with problem size, even when the problems are in fact simpler (fewer reasoning steps or shorter inputs). Finally, the third dimension the researchers explore is format generalisation, or how does the model respond to changes in the prompt such as altering elements by adding irrelevant information like extra letters or substituting a letter for another? In a real-world context, this would test how robust the model is to paraphrase, wording changes for an identical task. Worryingly, GPT-2 is very susceptible to these small changes. 

In conclusion, this paper highlights a fundamental limit of CoT prompting, which is that models reflect the data they are trained on. This is all the more crucial because prompting is a strategy for users who do not have access to the training data and thus cannot know whether their queries lie within the training data. It would be tempting to dismiss the model’s failure in POOD and OOD as blatantly “unfair” to the model: how could it know, for example, to shift letters by 13 characters in the alphabet if it has only ever seen rotations during its training. This is perfectly true but it is precisely the point that it is users who must be wary of assuming a model knows how to perform a certain type of reasoning task. More broadly, this paper also points to a fundamental difference between human reasoning, which can indeed generalise to novel tasks, and “reasoning” elicited from LLMs using CoT prompting. An important point to remember however is that CoT is only one ingredient for mimicking human reasoning. So-called Large Reasoning Models (LRM) for instance are specifically designed from the start to imitate human reasoning and may be more efficient than standard LLMs. A weaker criticism of this paper would be to target the abstract representation of tasks as element-transformation pairs as an adequate representation of human queries. Real-world questions are more variable, fuzzy and less mechanical as they do not consist in mere manipulation of input symbols. While true, I would argue that these tasks, which may seem highly abstract and almost mechanical are in fact simpler than real-world reasoning. Solving these should be the bare minimum to support a claim to reasoning.
